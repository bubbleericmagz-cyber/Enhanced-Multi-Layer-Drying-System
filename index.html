The problem with the traditional bilao

The traditional ground‑level bilao is central to food drying and display in Philippine markets—but it exposes vendors to high ergonomic risk and inconsistent drying conditions.

  • High musculoskeletal risk

    Frequent bending, lifting, and awkward postures lead to musculoskeletal disorders (MSDs), especially in the lower back, shoulders, and neck.

  • Weather‑dependent and exposed

    Products are vulnerable to sudden rain, moisture, and contaminants due to open exposure and lack of protective cover.

  • Space‑inefficient layouts

    Ground‑level trays occupy large floor areas, limiting movement in already congested market stalls.

Baseline findings

Using REBA, most vendors operating traditional bilaos fell in the High to Very High ergonomic risk range, calling for immediate design interventions.

Fieldwork conducted in Carbon Market, Cebu City, with active bilao users working 4–9 hours per day.

Enhanced multi-layer bilao drying system

The enhanced system reorganizes drying from the ground up—literally. A vertical, anthropometrically calibrated rack with automated motion and rain protection transforms how vendors dry, store, and display products.

01

Multi-layer vertical design

Stacked trays increase capacity while reducing floor space. Working heights are tuned using anthropometric data to minimize trunk flexion and awkward reach.

02

Ergonomic rack & handling

A stable rack and bilao holders support neutral postures and smoother handling, lowering physical strain during repetitive drying and display tasks.

03

Smart weather protection

An integrated rain sensor, automatic roll‑out shelf, and visual indicator help vendors react quickly to sudden weather changes, keeping products safe and dry.

Backed by ergonomic and anthropometric research

The enhanced multi-layer bilao system was evaluated through a quasi‑experimental, descriptive‑comparative study with experienced market vendors, using the Rapid Entire Body Assessment (REBA) tool and self‑reported discomfort scales.

Prototype status

The current prototype demonstrates the core ergonomic and functional improvements identified in the study—while leaving room for further optimization.

  • Working prototype

    Square‑tube frame with welded rack, Servomotor assembly, and multi‑layer bilao holders following documented work instructions.

  • Next design iterations

    Adjustable frame heights, higher‑capacity motor, transparent roof, industrial‑grade casters, and automatic shut‑off logic.

  • Deployment contexts

    Designed for small‑scale food processors and public market vendors handling fish, rice, coffee, and vegetables.
